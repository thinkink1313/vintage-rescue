Victorian Uranium & Green Glass

England & Bohemia, c. 1880s–1905. Uranium (“vaseline”) glows electric green under 365–395 nm UV. Non-uranium green is deeper emerald/olive and does not glow.

Davidson “Primrose Pearline” (Uranium)

England • c. 1889–1905 • Pressed glass with opalescent white rim
  • ID: Bright UV glow + milky pearline rim; crisp scroll/rib molds.
  • Marks: Sometimes British “Rd No.” on base; pattern variants exist.
  • Buy/Sell: Field buy $25–60 • Sell $100–180 (sets & large bowls do best).
Deep dive: history, traits, comps, watch-outs

Maker & history

George Davidson & Co. (Gateshead, UK) popularized “pearline” opalescent rims in the 1890s; primrose = yellow-green (uranium) line.

Key traits

Distinct opalescent white at edges; evenly glowing body under UV; well-finished feet; pressed seams but crisp.

Price comps

Clean serving bowls/handled baskets commonly $120–180; rarer forms higher. Heavy staining (“glass sickness”) reduces value.

Watch-outs

Clouding from dishwasher/chemicals; chips at rim beads; later non-uranium pearline exists (no glow).
Form: bowls, baskets, comports Fast flip: ✅ UV: strong electric green

Sowerby Pressed Uranium Plate

England • c. 1880s–1890s • Botanical/geom. patterns
  • ID: Even glow; sturdy weight; sharp mold detail; pattern numbers on some bases.
  • Buy/Sell: Field buy $40–90 • Sell $120–220 (multiples/sets increase value).
Deep dive

Maker

Sowerby (Gateshead) was a leading pressed-glass house; uranium batches used for showy tableware.

Traits

Consistent color; defined veining in botanical patterns; good base wear on authentic pieces.

Comps

Large serving platters/bread plates in strong patterns: up to ~$200+. Chips drop value.

Watch-outs

Mushy mold lines (worn molds or repro); no glow = non-uranium lookalike.
Form: plates, platters Fast flip: ✅ UV: strong, even

Thomas Webb & Sons — Cut/Etched Uranium

England • c. 1880s–1890s • Hand-blown, polished pontil
  • ID: Deep wheel-cut/acid-etched designs; high clarity; polished pontil with honest wear.
  • Buy/Sell: Field buy $120–300 • Sell $400–800+ (complex forms higher).
Deep dive

Maker

Webb was elite in English art glass; uranium batches used for luxury table and decorative wares.

Traits

Crisp cutting (not molded), balanced proportions, often slight color gradation in thicker areas.

Comps

Signed/attributed pieces with complex cutting can exceed $1k at auction; plain bowls less.

Watch-outs

Molded “fake cutting,” perfectly uniform machine facets, and freshly ground rims = red flags.
Higher value Form: bowls, vases UV: vivid

Victorian Uranium Epergne (Centerpiece)

England • c. 1880s–1900s • Multi-horn centerpiece, often with metal mount
  • ID: Multiple horns/trumpets; ruffled or crimped edges; strong UV glow.
  • Buy/Sell: Field buy $150–300 • Sell $500–900+ (complete, matched parts).
Deep dive

Context

Table centerpieces of the high Victorian taste; uranium examples are scarce on thrift shelves.

Traits

Matching glass color and edge treatments across horns; period metalwork; screws show age.

Comps

Complete sets with no damage command strong prices; single replacement horns much less.

Watch-outs

Mismatched replacement horns; tip chips; UV glow absent = non-uranium.
Rare on thrift shelves Form: centerpieces UV: strong

Uranium Goblet / Small Stem

UK/Bohemia • c. 1880s–1900s • Pressed or blown
  • ID: Bright edge/base glow; daylight color yellow-green; seams OK on pressed stems.
  • Buy/Sell: Field buy $15–40 • Sell $40–120+ (pairs/sets move faster).
Deep dive

Notes

Many workshops made utilitarian uranium stems; buy multiples when possible for better sell-through.

Watch-outs

Rim chips; dull non-glowing green (lookalike); overly light modern glass with no wear.
Fast flip: ✅ Form: goblets, cordials UV: strong

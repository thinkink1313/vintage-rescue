Davidson “Primrose Pearline” (Uranium)

England • c. 1889–1905 • Pressed glass with opalescent white rim
  • Fast ID: Milky pearline rim + strong UV glow; crisp pressed detail.
  • Marks: Some with “Rd No.” on base. Mold lines present but sharp.
  • Buy/Sell: Field buy $25–60 • Resell $100–180 (serving bowls/baskets do best).
Deep dive: maker, tells, comps, watch-outs

Maker & context

George Davidson & Co. (Gateshead) popularized opalescent “pearline” rims in the 1890s; Primrose is the yellow-green uranium line.

Authentic tells

Even uranium glow; opalescent rim fades into body; substantial weight; feet well finished.

Comps & value

Clean baskets/large bowls $120–180; rare forms higher. Stained / sick glass drops sharply.

Watch-outs

Dishwasher clouding; bead-rim chips; later non-uranium runs (no UV glow) can look similar.
Forms: bowls, baskets, comports UV: strong electric green Fast flip: ✅

Sowerby Pressed Uranium Plate

England • c. 1880s–1890s • Botanical & geometric molds
  • Fast ID: Even glow; sturdy weight; sharp patterning; some bases show pattern numbers.
  • Buy/Sell: Field buy $40–90 • Resell $120–220 (sets command more).
Deep dive: what to check

Maker

Sowerby (Gateshead) excelled at high-detail pressed patterns; uranium batches were showy tableware.

Authentic tells

Crisp veins in leaves; good base wear; consistent wall thickness (not paper-thin).

Comps

Large platters/bread plates with strong patterns reach $180–220; chips cut that in half.

Watch-outs

Mushy detail = worn molds or repro; no glow = non-uranium green look-alike.
Forms: plates, platters UV: strong, even Fast flip: ✅

Thomas Webb & Sons — Cut/Etched Uranium

England • c. 1880s–1890s • Hand-blown, polished pontil
  • Fast ID: Wheel-cut/acid-etched (not molded); high clarity; polished pontil with honest wear.
  • Buy/Sell: Field buy $120–300 • Resell $400–800+ (complex forms can exceed $1k).
Deep dive: authenticity & pitfalls

Authentic tells

Crisp hand-cut motifs; slight color gradation at thick points; balanced proportions; ground/polished foot.

Red flags

Molded “fake-cut” facets, razor-uniform machine cuts, fresh ground rims on otherwise old glass.

Care

Avoid dishwashers; lukewarm hand wash only to preserve sheen and avoid “sickness.”
Higher value Forms: bowls, vases UV: vivid

Victorian Uranium Epergne (Centerpiece)

England • c. 1880s–1900s • Multi-horn centerpiece, often with metal mount
  • Fast ID: Multiple matching horns; ruffled/crimped edges; strong UV glow.
  • Buy/Sell: Field buy $150–300 • Resell $500–900+ (complete, matched parts).
Deep dive: how to check completeness

Completeness

Verify all horns match color/edge treatment; look for period screws and aged metalwork.

Value factors

Complete + no chips = premium; single replacement horns sell lower but still desirable.

Watch-outs

Mismatched parts and no UV glow; hairline cracks at horn sockets are common—inspect closely.
Rare on thrift shelves Forms: centerpieces UV: strong

Uranium Goblet / Small Stem

UK/Bohemia • c. 1880s–1900s • Pressed or blown
  • Fast ID: Bright base/edge glow; yellow-green daylight color; pressed seams OK.
  • Buy/Sell: Field buy $15–40 • Resell $40–120+ (pairs/sets move faster).
Deep dive: buying strategy

Notes

Great entry uranium; prioritize clean rims and multiples; check for proper wear on feet.

Watch-outs

Rim nicks; dull non-glowing green; ultra-light modern glass with no age wear.
Fast flip: ✅ Forms: goblets, cordials UV: strong

Lookalike: Victorian Green (Non-Uranium)

UK/Bohemia • c. 1870s–1890s • Pressed soda-lime glass
  • Fast ID: Deeper emerald/olive; heavier feel; no UV glow; softer mold lines.
  • Buy/Sell: Field buy $5–20 • Resell $20–60 (decorative; ID pattern to do better).
Deep dive: why it fools people

Context

Very common on shelves; easy to confuse with uranium in daylight. Always test with 365–395 nm UV.

Watch-outs

Don’t overpay; value rises only when tied to a sought pattern/maker and in excellent condition.
